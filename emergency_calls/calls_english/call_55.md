# EmergencyCall_55

Dialog

- calltaker: Emergency dispatch for fire and ambulance, in which town is the emergency?

- caller: Um… in [insert city], in a psychiatric day clinic… I’m totally confused right now.

- calltaker: Which ward/unit are you in?

- caller: I… I don’t know exactly what the ward is called… we’re in the day clinic, in the therapy room, a group room with chairs in a circle. Can you find it by the address?

- calltaker: You’re not staff?

- caller: No, I’m a patient here, not staff… the therapist is here too.

- calltaker: Who is affected?

- caller: A man, also a patient here, maybe mid-thirties… he suddenly went very pale and says he’s extremely dizzy and that he’s about to pass out.

- calltaker: What happened?

- caller: He stood up in the group, then immediately said everything was spinning and his hands were tingling… he grabbed the chair and is breathing very fast. When he sits down it gets a little better briefly; when he stands up it comes back.

- calltaker: Is he normally responsive?

- caller: Yes, yes, he’s awake and answering, but totally panicked and keeps saying he’s going to faint.

- calltaker: How is his breathing? Can he breathe adequately?

- caller: He’s getting air, but he’s breathing way too fast, really frantic… like gasping for air, but he says he can breathe. No blue lips or anything.

- calltaker: Any chest pain?

- caller: He says no chest pain… more like a pressure sensation and that he feels nauseous because everything is spinning.

- calltaker: Where is the pressure?

- caller: Um… he’s pointing to the center of his chest, but he doesn’t describe it as a sharp pain, more like “weirdly tight,” and that his heart is racing.

- calltaker: Okay. I’m sending an ambulance. Does he have preexisting conditions?

- caller: He apparently has something like panic [disorder]… he mentioned that before, and he takes some antidepressant, like one tablet every day. I don’t know anything else, no heart problems as far as I know.

- calltaker: Okay. Try to calm him. It’s important that he slows his breathing.

- caller: Okay, yes… I’ll try. I’m telling him to breathe in and out slowly with me… but he’s shaking a lot and his hands are kind of tense/cramped. Should I lay him down or keep him sitting?

- calltaker: It’s best that he stays sitting. You can try to coach his breathing, i.e., breathe slowly with him.

- caller: Okay, he’s sitting now, back against the chair… I’m breathing with him slowly. It’s a minimal improvement, I think, but he still says he’s dizzy and his fingers are tingling.

- calltaker: Very good, keep doing that. The colleagues are almost there. If anything changes, call me again.

- caller: Yes… okay. I’ll stay with him. If he actually collapses or something, I’ll call right away.
