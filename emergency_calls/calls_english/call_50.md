# Emergency_Call_50

## Dialogue

- calltaker: Fire and Rescue Emergency Service, in which location is the emergency?

- caller: Uh… in the city library here in Münster, downtown! Please, this is urgent, someone here just collapsed.

- calltaker: Who is it?

- caller: A woman, late thirties maybe… she was at some reading and suddenly slid off the chair and fell over. She keeps holding the back of her head and seems totally dazed.

- calltaker: Is she currently responsive?

- caller: Only partially… if you speak loudly to her she sometimes opens her eyes briefly, but her speech is very odd, kind of slurred, and she’s looking off to the right. Then she gets very sleepy again.

- calltaker: Can she breathe normally?

- caller: Yes, she’s breathing, but somehow labored and irregular… and she vomited earlier, there’s still something at her mouth. We put her on her side so she doesn’t choke on it.

- calltaker: Is her breathing adequate?

- caller: I… I think so, her chest is moving up and down, but it doesn’t look calm, more like gasping. She’s also going very pale, that really scares me.

- calltaker: Okay. Is she currently in the recovery position?

- caller: Yes, exactly, two people are holding her in the recovery position, head a bit lower so the vomit can run out. We also loosened her scarf.

- calltaker: Very good. Did she complain about anything beforehand?

- caller: Yes, about ten minutes ago she suddenly said she had very severe headache, “like being hit,” and touched the back of her head. Then she felt sick and vomited once, and shortly after that she just slid away.

- calltaker: Is there anyone who knows her?

- caller: Yes, the librarian says he knows her by sight, and he has her wallet—there’s a list of medications in there. It says something for migraine and a little pill, I think.

- calltaker: Okay, but they don’t know of any other pre-existing conditions?

- caller: Only that she apparently has migraines, but people say it was never this bad before. Otherwise… no idea, no diabetes or anything, nobody here knows.

- calltaker: Okay. The ambulance crew is on their way. It is important that you continue to watch whether she breathes. If she stops breathing you must begin cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR), understood?

- caller: Yes, yes, okay… we’re watching all the time. Please come quickly, she really doesn’t look well.

- calltaker: Good. If anything changes or she stops breathing call 112 again, okay?

- caller: Yes, I will! Wait—oh God, she… she’s just getting very stiff, like her whole body, the arms… it’s happening… what should I do?!

- calltaker: Is she seizing?

- caller: Yes! She’s convulsing totally, really rigid, and she’s making these strange noises like she can’t breathe… it’s been about… 10, 15 seconds… oh man. We’re just holding her so she doesn’t hit the shelf.

- calltaker: Is she still convulsing now?

- caller: No, it’s letting up now… she’s completely limp again, eyes half-closed, barely responsive. She’s breathing again, but very deep and slow… and there’s still saliva at the corner of her mouth.

- calltaker: Then place her again in the recovery position if you can.

- caller: Yes, we’re doing that—okay, she’s lying stable on her side again. I’m just gently wiping her mouth clear… she’s so groggy, doesn’t really answer anymore. Please, are they almost there?

- calltaker: The crew is just around the corner. I will stay on the phone until they have located you.

- caller: Okay… we’re almost at the entrance, but still in an aisle between the stacks, it’s narrow here. I’ll tell someone to guide the paramedics in, okay?

- calltaker: Exactly.

- caller: All right—hey you there, go to the door and wave them in! …Oh God, she’s still breathing, yes, but she’s totally out of it… please hurry.
