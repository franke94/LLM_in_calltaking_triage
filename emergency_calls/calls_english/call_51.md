Emergency Call_51

Dialog

- calltaker: Fire and rescue emergency line, in which location is the emergency?

- caller: In the swimming facility in Münster, at the Hallenbad Mitte… uh, I don’t know the exact street, but it’s very crowded here, please hurry!

- calltaker: What happened?

- caller: A man, about mid-30s, he was doing breath‑hold training in the pool and when he came up he suddenly could not breathe. He’s coughing a lot and spitting up pink, frothy material, and he’s already turning quite blue in the face.

- calltaker: But he’s out of the water now?

- caller: Yes, yes, he’s out! The lifeguards pulled him to the edge and now he’s lying in a first‑aid area next to the pool. He’s still gasping for air.

- calltaker: Is he responsive?

- caller: Yes… well, he looks at me, but he can hardly speak, only single words like “air…” and then he coughs again. He seems totally panicked and exhausted.

- calltaker: Okay, so he’s conscious but the problem is that he’s not getting adequate air?

- caller: Exactly, he just can’t get air in, like it won’t go in. He’s breathing very fast and shallow and coughing continuously, and that frothy stuff keeps coming up.

- calltaker: Do you hear any unusual noises when he breathes?

- caller: Yes, like a real rattling, as if there’s water or foam in there… and sometimes a wheeze. You can hear it even with the noise here.

- calltaker: Is he reporting pain?

- caller: He’s just shaking his head, he can hardly say anything… he’s more holding his chest like it’s tight or pressing, but he didn’t really say “pain.” Mainly severe shortness of breath.

- calltaker: Okay, the team is already on the way and hurrying.

- caller: Please, yes… he’s getting bluer, it really scares me. The lifeguards already put an oxygen mask on him, but it’s not really helping.

- calltaker: Okay, the oxygen mask is good. The team is en route.

- caller: Okay… should we do anything else? He’s coughing so hard, he’s all sweaty and clammy and clinging to the lounge.

- calltaker: The oxygen mask should be set to a high flow.

- caller: Okay, I’ll tell the lifeguard… hold on… yes, he’s turning it up now, it’s already fairly open. But the man is still gasping and coughing up foam, it’s not stopping.

- calltaker: Yes, the crew will be there in a few minutes. If anything worsens—if he stops breathing—call us again, okay. It’s best to send someone out to meet and direct the crew.

- caller: Yes, I will! I’ll send his training partner out to the entrance to wave. I’ll stay here with him… if he collapses I’ll call again immediately.
